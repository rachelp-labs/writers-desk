Glossary

AI terms for medical writers

Large Language Model LLM
An AI model trained on large volumes of text to generate, summarise, and transform language. Examples include GPT-4 and Claude. In medical writing, LLMs are used to draft sections, summarise data, and assist with literature review — but require expert review for accuracy and regulatory appropriateness.
Hallucination
When an LLM generates content that is plausible-sounding but factually incorrect or fabricated. In a regulatory context this is a critical risk — citations, patient numbers, and statistical values generated by AI must always be independently verified against source documents.
Prompt Engineering
The practice of crafting inputs to an AI model to produce more accurate, relevant, or structured outputs. For medical writers, effective prompting includes specifying document type, audience, regulatory context, and desired format.
Retrieval-Augmented Generation RAG
A technique that grounds an LLM's responses in a specific document set — for example, a clinical study report or protocol — rather than relying solely on training data. Reduces hallucination risk by anchoring outputs to verified source material.
Generative AI
AI systems capable of generating new content — text, images, or data — based on patterns learned during training. Distinct from AI used purely for classification or prediction. Most AI writing tools used in pharma are generative.
Fine-tuning
Additional training of an existing AI model on a specific dataset to improve performance in a particular domain. A model fine-tuned on regulatory documents may perform better on clinical writing tasks than a general-purpose model.
Human-in-the-loop
A workflow in which a human reviews and validates AI outputs before they are used or submitted. Considered best practice — and in many cases a regulatory expectation — for AI-assisted medical writing.
Token
The unit of text an LLM processes — roughly equivalent to a word or word fragment. Models have a maximum context window measured in tokens, which determines how much text can be processed in a single interaction. Relevant when working with long clinical documents.
